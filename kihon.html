Kihon

The grammar of karate. Every kata, every kumite exchange, every grading — all of it is built from the small set of basics on this page. They are the first thing learned and the last thing perfected.

Tachi · Tsuki · Geri · Uke — Stances · Punches · Kicks · Blocks

立ち方 — Tachi-kata

Stances

A stance is not a posture; it is a moment in the transfer of weight. Hold a stance like you might be moved out of it at any instant — because in real practice, you will be.

Zenkutsu-dachi · 前屈立ち

Front stance

Weight: 60 / 40 forward Length: ~2 shoulder widths Front knee: bent over toes

The principal forward-power stance. Hips are square; rear leg is straight; front knee bends so a vertical line drops from the kneecap through the front toes. Used for committed forward attacks (oi-zuki, gyaku-zuki) and most transitions in kata.

Common error: rear heel rising (loses ground). Drill: step forward and back the length of the dōjō, repeatedly, holding hikite tight.

Kōkutsu-dachi · 後屈立ち

Back stance

Weight: 70 / 30 rear Feet: 90° L-shape Length: ~1.5 shoulder widths

Rear-weighted stance for receiving and returning. The rear foot is perpendicular to the front; the rear knee is bent and aligned over the rear foot. Almost every shutō-uke in Shōrin-Ryū kata is performed in kōkutsu-dachi.

Common error: front foot drifting outward — keep both knees tracking over their respective toes.

Kiba-dachi · 騎馬立ち (also Naihanchi-dachi)

Horse stance

Weight: 50 / 50 Width: ~2 shoulder widths Knees: pushed outward

The most powerful side-on stance, named for the posture of a horseback rider. Naihanchi-dachi (the version held throughout the Naihanchi kata) is slightly narrower with the toes turned in. Builds leg endurance, lateral stability, and the kagi-zuki / hook-punch foundation.

Drill: hold for two full minutes. When the legs shake, hold longer. Motobu Chōki is reported to have held it for an hour at a time.

Neko-ashi-dachi · 猫足立ち

Cat stance

Weight: 90 / 10 rear Front foot: ball only, heel raised Length: ~1 shoulder width

The "cat foot" stance. Almost all weight on the rear leg, the front foot resting like a paw — instantly available to kick (mae-geri keage), or to retreat. Used in defensive sequences, especially in Pinan, Rōhai, and Chinto.

Common error: dropping weight onto the front foot. Test by lifting the front knee — you should not need to shift weight first.

Sanchin-dachi · 三戦立ち

Hourglass stance

Weight: 50 / 50 Toes: turned in Knees: squeezed in

"Three battles" stance. Toes and knees turn inward to form an hourglass — locks the centerline, protects the groin, roots against pushes. Imported from Goju lineage but appears in Shōrin-flavored kata like Seisan and the advanced Sanchin practice itself. Trained with synchronized breathing (ibuki).

Heikō-dachi · 平行立ち · Hachiji-dachi · 八字立ち · Musubi-dachi · 結び立ち

Ready & bowing stances

Three closely related neutral stances:

  • Heikō-dachi — feet shoulder-width, parallel. The default "yoi" / ready stance.
  • Hachiji-dachi — same width, toes turned outward to ~30° (forms the kanji 八). Slightly more relaxed.
  • Musubi-dachi — heels together, toes apart at 45°. Used for formal bowing (rei).

Stance principles common to all

  • Weight on the balls of the feet, not the heels — but heels stay grounded.
  • Knees track over toes in their direction — never collapse inward (except sanchin).
  • Hara (centre) is dropped — the spine remains vertical; the seat lowers.
  • Hikite is alive — the pulling hand is always in tension, ready to attack.
  • The stance is the foundation, the upper body is the brush — strikes only carry power if the stance is rooted.
突き — Tsuki / Zuki

Punches

Power flows from the back foot, through the rotating hip, into the punching arm. The pulling hand (hikite) is half the punch.

Oi-zuki · 追い突き

Lunge punch

Stance: zenkutsu Same-side punching hand & forward foot

The committing forward punch — the body lunges with the strike. Used in the basics line and as the opening of dozens of kata. Times the punch's impact with the front foot's planting; the rear foot drives, the hip rotates.

Drill: walk the floor 20 times in oi-zuki. Listen for the gi snap on every punch — no snap, no kime.

Gyaku-zuki · 逆突き

Reverse punch

Stance: zenkutsu Opposite-side punching hand & forward foot

The principal scoring punch in sport karate, and the standard counter after a block. The rear hand is the weapon, but the engine is the hip — without rotation, gyaku-zuki is just a push. The forward hand rips back to hikite at the same moment.

Practice: sets of 10 in zenkutsu, alternating gedan-barai → gyaku-zuki, both sides.

Kizami-zuki · 刻み突き

Jab

Lead-hand punch, no full hip rotation. Fast, used to set up combinations or measure ma-ai.

Ura-zuki · 裏突き

Close upside-down punch

Short-range punch with palm facing upward — like an uppercut without the rise. Common in Naihanchi.

Tate-zuki · 縦突き

Vertical fist

Punch ends with the fist vertical, thumb up. Natural alignment for the wrist; common in Shōrin kata.

Age-zuki · 上げ突き

Rising punch

An upward strike from below, targeting the jaw or sternum. Power comes from rising legs and rotating shoulders.

Kagi-zuki · 鉤突き

Hook punch

A hook across the body — signature of Naihanchi. Elbow stays at 90°; power from the shoulder pivot.

Yama-zuki · 山突き

Mountain punch

Two punches at once — high and low — landing simultaneously like the peaks of a mountain. Iconic finish in Passai Dai.

Three properties of every punch

  1. Form (kata) — wrist straight, first two knuckles strike, elbow nearly extended (not locked).
  2. Focus (kime) — the entire body tenses for one instant at impact, then releases.
  3. Path (keiro) — the punch travels the shortest line from hikite to target. No drift, no telegraph.
蹴り — Geri

Kicks

Okinawan karate keeps kicks low and grounded. The hip is loaded by the supporting leg; the kicking foot is a hammer, not a flag.

Mae-geri (keage / kekomi) · 前蹴り

Front kick

Striking surface: koshi (ball of foot), toes pulled up

Keage — snap kick: hip rises, knee snaps the foot up and back. Kekomi — thrust kick: hip drives forward, foot pushes through. The foundational kick of every kata; also the most under-trained.

Yoko-geri keage / kekomi · 横蹴り

Side kick

Striking surface: sokutō (knife edge of foot)

Side snap or side thrust. Hip rolls, supporting foot pivots so the heel points at the target. Keage snaps up to head height; kekomi drives horizontally to ribs or knee.

Mawashi-geri · 回し蹴り

Round kick

Striking surface: haisoku (instep) or sune (shin)

The kick travels in a horizontal arc — knee chambered to the side, foot whips through. The supporting foot pivots fully so the hips can finish square to the target. Most-used scoring kick in modern competition.

Ushiro-geri · 後ろ蹴り

Back kick

Striking surface: kakato (heel)

A linear thrust kick to the rear. Eyes turn first, then the kicking knee drives toward the chest, then the heel fires backward. Used against opponents at the back; appears in advanced kata.

受け — Uke

Blocks (receivings)

Uke means "to receive," not "to block." A block in Okinawan karate is rarely a hard stop — it is a parry, a deflection, an interception, sometimes itself a strike. Train them as live engagements.

Age-uke · 上げ受け

Rising block

Forearm rises diagonally above the head, deflecting a downward attack to the side. Forearm angles ~45°, fist past the temple, opposite hand rips to hikite.

Soto-uke · 外受け

Outside-in block

Forearm sweeps from outside the body inward, deflecting a chest-level punch to the centerline. Often paired with a counter-gyaku-zuki on the same beat.

Uchi-uke · 内受け

Inside-out block

Mirror of soto-uke — forearm sweeps from inside the body outward. Excellent for parrying a punch and immediately trapping the attacker's arm.

Gedan-barai · 下段払い

Low sweeping block

Sweeps a low attack (kick or grab) outward and downward. Opens almost every Shōrin-Ryū kata. The blocking arm finishes a fist's width above the front knee.

Shutō-uke · 手刀受け

Knife-hand block

Open-hand block, performed almost exclusively in kōkutsu-dachi. The blocking hand cuts down/across; the supporting hand sits palm-up at the solar plexus, ready to grab or strike.

Jūji-uke · 十字受け (also Kosa-uke)

X-block

Wrists cross to form a "+", trapping a downward weapon strike or a kick. The cross can immediately rotate into a grab. Pinan Yondan signature.

Uke is half the strike

The Okinawan masters often said: "Block and counter on the same beat." A block performed without an immediate counter is only half the technique. In every basic combination — gedan-barai → gyaku-zuki, age-uke → gyaku-zuki, shutō-uke → nukite — the block creates the opening for the counter.

基本組み合わせ — Kihon kumiawase

OSK basics combinations — PT 1 & PT 2

The OSK Academy syllabus organises basics into two structured sequences. PT 1 walks the body through the base techniques up and down the floor; PT 2 (Shenshin Gōtai) chains them into combinations forward and back.

PT 1 — fundamentals (15–27 moves)

The first sequence isolates each technique. Performed up and back, it is your daily warm-up and your last revision before grading.

  1. Yoi (heikō-dachi)
  2. Step forward, gedan-barai (zenkutsu)
  3. Oi-zuki chūdan
  4. Oi-zuki jōdan
  5. Step forward, age-uke + gyaku-zuki
  6. Step forward, soto-uke + gyaku-zuki
  7. Step forward, uchi-uke + gyaku-zuki
  8. Step back, shutō-uke (kōkutsu) + nukite (zenkutsu)
  9. Mae-geri (front leg, then rear)
  10. Yoko-geri keage (kiba-dachi)
  11. Yoko-geri kekomi (kiba-dachi)
  12. Mawashi-geri
  13. Ushiro-geri
  14. Naore (return to yoi)

PT 2 — Shenshin Gōtai (5–17 combinations)

"Body and spirit unified." The second sequence chains the PT 1 vocabulary into responsive combinations — block-counter, kick-punch-block, retreat-engage. Performed both forward and back, both sides.

  1. Sanbon-zuki — jōdan, chūdan, chūdan
  2. Age-uke → gyaku-zuki → mae-geri
  3. Soto-uke → empi → uraken → gyaku-zuki
  4. Mae-geri → yoko-geri → mawashi-geri (without setting down)
  5. Step back gedan-barai → kizami-zuki → gyaku-zuki
  6. Shutō-uke (kōkutsu) → mae-geri → nukite (zenkutsu)
  7. Uchi-uke → kizami-zuki → gyaku-zuki
  8. Jūji-uke (gedan) → rising punch → throw setup
  9. Mawashi-uke → morote-zuki (Goju-flavored close)
  10. Naore

How to drill PT 1 / PT 2 alone

Set a timer for 20 minutes. Perform PT 1 four times: once for shape (slow), once for breath (slow with kiai), once for speed, once with eyes closed (no peeking — root from the floor up). Then PT 2 once each side. End with two minutes in kiba-dachi.
