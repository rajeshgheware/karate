立ち方 — Tachi-kata

Stances

A stance is a moment in the transfer of weight, not a fixed posture — hold it ready to be moved out of it at any instant.

United Shōrin-Ryū Karate · stance demonstration

Zenkutsu-dachi · 前屈立ち

Front stance

The principal forward-power stance. Hips square, rear leg straight, front knee bent over the toes; weight ~60/40 forward. Drives committed attacks (oi-zuki, gyaku-zuki). Common error: rear heel rising.

Kōkutsu-dachi · 後屈立ち

Back stance

Rear-weighted (~70/30), feet in a 90° L-shape. Almost every shutō-uke in Shōrin-Ryū kata is performed here. Keep both knees tracking over their toes.

Kiba-dachi · 騎馬立ち

Horse stance

50/50, knees pushed outward, ~2 shoulder-widths wide. Naihanchi-dachi is the narrower, toes-in version held through Naihanchi. Builds leg endurance and the kagi-zuki foundation. Hold past the shaking.

Neko-ashi-dachi · 猫足立ち

Cat stance

~90/10 on the rear leg, front foot resting on the ball with the heel raised — instantly free to kick (mae-geri keage) or retreat. Test it by lifting the front knee without shifting weight first.

Sanchin-dachi · 三戦立ち

Hourglass stance

"Three battles." Toes and knees turn inward to lock the centerline, protect the groin, and root against pushes. From the Goju line; appears in Seisan. Trained with synchronized breathing (ibuki).

Heikō · Hachiji · Musubi-dachi

Ready & bowing stances

Heikō — feet parallel, shoulder-width (the default yoi). Hachiji — toes turned out ~30° (forms 八). Musubi — heels together, toes 45°, for formal bowing (rei).

Also on the shodan stance list

The grading syllabus names a few more stances by their OSK spelling — listed and video-illustrated on the Grading page. In short:

  • Shiko-dachi — like kiba-dachi but toes and knees turned out ~45°, sat lower.
  • Seisan-dachi — the rooted stance of the Seisan kata; shorter than zenkutsu, both knees bent. (listed as "Sheshenchin" — verify with Sensei)
  • Heisoku-dachi — feet together, parallel; a closed formal stance. (listed as "Isko" — verify with Sensei)
  • Gedan-barai-dachi — the ready posture from which gedan-barai is set. (verify with Sensei)

Stance principles common to all

  • Weight on the balls of the feet, not the heels — but heels stay grounded.
  • Knees track over toes in their direction — never collapse inward (except sanchin).
  • Hara (centre) is dropped — the spine stays vertical; the seat lowers.
  • Hikite is alive — the pulling hand is always in tension, ready to attack.
突き — Tsuki / Zuki

Punches

Power flows from the back foot, through the rotating hip, into the punching arm. The pulling hand (hikite) is half the punch.

Richard Yamato · Okinawa Shōrin-Ryū — basic punches

Oi-zuki · 追い突き

Lunge punch

Same-side hand and foot lunge with the strike in zenkutsu. Time impact to the front foot planting — rear foot drives, hip rotates.

Gyaku-zuki · 逆突き

Reverse punch

Opposite-side hand; the standard counter after a block and the main scoring punch. The rear hand is the weapon, but the hip is the engine — without rotation it is just a push.

Kizami-zuki · 刻み突き

Jab

Lead-hand punch, no full hip rotation. Fast — used to set up combinations or measure ma-ai.

Ura-zuki · 裏突き

Close upside-down punch

Short-range punch with the palm facing up — like an uppercut without the rise. Common in Naihanchi.

Tate-zuki · 縦突き

Vertical fist

Ends with the fist vertical, thumb up — a natural wrist alignment. Common in Shōrin kata.

Age-zuki · 上げ突き

Rising punch

An upward strike from below to the jaw or sternum. Power from rising legs and rotating shoulders.

Kagi-zuki · 鉤突き

Hook punch

A hook across the body — signature of Naihanchi. Elbow held at 90°; power from the shoulder pivot.

Yama-zuki · 山突き

Mountain punch

Two punches at once, high and low, landing together like a mountain's peaks. Iconic finish in Passai Dai.

Three properties of every punch

  1. Form (kata) — wrist straight, first two knuckles strike, elbow nearly extended (not locked).
  2. Focus (kime) — the whole body tenses for one instant at impact, then releases.
  3. Path (keiro) — the punch travels the shortest line from hikite to target. No drift, no telegraph.
蹴り — Geri

Kicks

Okinawan kicks stay low and grounded — the supporting leg loads the hip; the kicking foot is a hammer, not a flag.

Okinawa Shōrin-Ryū — kicking demonstration

Mae-geri · 前蹴り

Front kick

Strikes with koshi (ball of foot), toes pulled up. Keage — snap: knee snaps the foot up and back. Kekomi — thrust: the hip drives the foot through. The foundation kick of every kata.

Yoko-geri · 横蹴り

Side kick

Strikes with sokutō (knife-edge of foot). The supporting foot pivots so the heel points at the target. Keage snaps up high; kekomi drives horizontally to ribs or knee.

Mawashi-geri · 回し蹴り

Round kick

A horizontal arc — knee chambered to the side, foot whipping through with haisoku (instep) or sune (shin); supporting foot pivots fully. The most-used scoring kick in competition.

Ushiro-geri · 後ろ蹴り

Back kick

A linear thrust to the rear with kakato (heel). Eyes turn first, the kicking knee drives toward the chest, then the heel fires backward.

受け — Uke

Blocks (receivings)

Uke means "to receive," not "to block." An Okinawan block is rarely a hard stop — a parry, a deflection, an interception, sometimes itself a strike. Train them live.

Karate Dōjō waKu · Matsubayashi Shōrin-Ryū, Okinawa — blocks

Age-uke · 上げ受け

Rising block

The forearm rises diagonally ~45° above the head, deflecting a downward attack aside. Fist past the temple; the opposite hand rips to hikite.

Soto-uke · 外受け

Outside-in block

The forearm sweeps from outside the body inward, deflecting a chest punch to the centerline. Often paired with a counter-gyaku-zuki on the same beat.

Uchi-uke · 内受け

Inside-out block

Mirror of soto-uke — the forearm sweeps from inside the body outward. Excellent for parrying a punch and immediately trapping the arm.

Gedan-barai · 下段払い

Low sweeping block

Sweeps a low attack (kick or grab) outward and downward. Opens almost every Shōrin-Ryū kata. The arm finishes a fist's width above the front knee.

Shutō-uke · 手刀受け

Knife-hand block

Open-hand block, performed almost exclusively in kōkutsu-dachi. The supporting hand sits palm-up at the solar plexus, ready to grab or strike.

Jūji-uke · 十字受け

X-block

The wrists cross to trap a downward strike or a kick. The cross can rotate straight into a grab. A Pinan Yondan signature. (also Kosa-uke)

Uke is half the strike

The Okinawan masters often said: "Block and counter on the same beat." A block without an immediate counter is only half the technique. In every basic combination — gedan-barai → gyaku-zuki, age-uke → gyaku-zuki, shutō-uke → nukite — the block creates the opening for the counter.

基本組み合わせ — Kihon kumiawase

OSK basics combinations — PT 1 & PT 2

The OSK Academy syllabus organises basics into two structured sequences. PT 1 (Kihon Renshu) walks the body through 27 base techniques up and down the floor; PT 2 (Shinshin Kotai — "advancing & retreating") chains them into 17 combinations forward and back. The full numbered lists, each video-illustrated, live on the Grading page.

PT 1 — Kihon Renshu (27 basics)

The first sequence isolates each technique — your daily warm-up and your last revision before grading. It runs through the punches (chūdan / jōdan / gedan / yoko / ushiro), the blocks (jōdan, chūdan, uchi, shutō), the kicks (mae, yoko, ushiro, mawashi), ashi-barai, nukite, and the consecutive and turning variations.

See all 27, with video

PT 2 — Shinshin Kotai (17 combinations)

"Advancing and retreating." The second sequence chains the PT 1 vocabulary into responsive combinations — block-counter, kick-punch-block, retreat-engage — performed forward and back, both sides. It builds toward PT 3 (Ippon Kumite), the one-step partner drills.

See all 17 + PT 3, with video

How to drill PT 1 / PT 2 alone

Set a timer for 20 minutes. Perform PT 1 four times: once for shape (slow), once for breath (slow with kiai), once for speed, once with eyes closed (no peeking — root from the floor up). Then PT 2 once each side. End with two minutes in kiba-dachi.
