道のり — Michi-nori

The remaining road

Phase 1 · Mar – May 2026

Consolidate brown 2. Sharpen Pinan series + Naihanchi Shodan + Passai Shō. Begin Passai Dai. Daily kihon. Introduce Naihanchi Nidan.

Phase 2 · Jun – Aug 2026 → Brown 3

Press to 1st kyū. Add Seisan, Ananku, Gojūshiho. Bunkai for one Pinan + Naihanchi. Bō: begin Shūshi-no-Kon. Jiyū-ippon kumite.

Phase 3 · Sep 2026 – grading

Shodan preparation. Add Chinto and Kūsankū. Polish all kata for examiner. Tokui-gata chosen. Kumite gauntlet drilled. Oral & written prep.

Three principles for self-practice

  1. Less reps, deeper attention. Five perfect mae-geri beat fifty sloppy ones. The body learns what you actually do, not what you intend.
  2. Always end with the basics. The last technique of every session should be something you have done a thousand times — leave with confirmation, not novelty.
  3. Train one thing alone, not everything together. Daily focus on one stance, one block, or one kata. The other domains rest while one progresses.
鍛錬 — Tanren

Conditioning for shodan

The shodan gauntlet — 60–90 minutes of full-effort kihon, multiple kata, and consecutive jiyū kumite — is as much a conditioning test as a technical one. Begin training the body for it now.

The fitness baseline

Aim for these by 6 months before grading:

  • Cardio: 5 km run in < 30 min, or 30 min continuous shadow-kumite without pause.
  • Strength: 50 push-ups (military), 50 sit-ups, 30 burpees in 3 minutes.
  • Stance endurance: kiba-dachi held for 5 minutes; zenkutsu held for 3 each side.
  • Kicking endurance: 100 mae-geri each leg without setting down.
  • Flexibility: mawashi-geri to head height (front leg) without warm-up; full splits-progress over years.

Hojo undō rotation

Three sessions per week, 30–40 min each. Pick two tools per session.

Makiwara50 oi-zuki + 50 gyaku-zuki each hand. Build to 100. Knuckles must callus, not bleed.
Chi-ishiWrist circles, arc swings, single-arm presses. 3×10 each pattern.
Nigiri-gameWalk in sanchin-dachi 3×30 m holding the jars by the rim. Forearms will burn.
Ishi-sashiSubstitute kettlebell. Goblet squats, swings, halos — 3×10.
Tetsu-getaOr ankle weights. Slow mae-geri × 30 each leg, no setting down.

Mobility & recovery

  • Daily: 10 min joint circles (neck, shoulders, hips, knees, ankles). No karate without this.
  • Twice weekly: 20 min static stretching after class. Front splits, side splits, hip openers.
  • Weekly: long walk (45+ min), or yoga session. Active recovery.
  • Sleep: 7+ hours. The body integrates training in deep sleep, not on the floor.

Nutrition & weight

  • Train light — empty stomach for early kata, light snack 60 min before class.
  • Hydrate before and during; coconut water or electrolytes after.
  • Protein within 30 min after every session (eggs, dal, lentils, paneer, fish).
  • If your weight class matters for tournament kumite — discuss with sensei. Most Okinawan schools do not weigh-in.
精神修養 — Seishin shūyō

Mental preparation

A grading is not a fight; it is an examination of who you have become through training. The right mental frame for the year ahead is not "I must pass" — it is "I will arrive ready, and the result will be honest."

初心 · Shoshin

Beginner's mind

Approach every class as if it is the first. The brown belt who has "seen this before" learns nothing new; the brown belt who has "never seen this before" sees everything.

残心 · Zanshin

Lingering awareness

Train zanshin in daily life: do not collapse posture after each task. Examiners watch what you do after a technique as much as the technique itself.

忍耐 · Nintai

Patient endurance

The plateau before shodan is normal — and the plateau is where the real shodan is forged. Skill compounds invisibly until one day it is visible.

不動心 · Fudōshin

Immovable mind

Practice composure under stress: train when tired, train in front of strangers, train with a sempai watching. The grading must feel familiar, not extraordinary.

継続 · Keizoku

Continuation

"Keizoku wa chikara nari" — continuation is power. Three poor sessions per week will outpace one perfect session per month.

空 · Kū

Empty

The day of the exam: empty the head. No counting moves, no rehearsing the next combination. Trust what training has put into the body. Watch what arises.

審査 — Shinsa

The shodan examination

A half-day to full-day event with senior examiners (often visiting from other dōjō) and an audience. OSK Academy's exact format is set by Renshi Sajesh and Kyōshi Chandran — verify two months out — but the components below are near-universal.

Component 1 · Opening

Reigi & line-up

Formal seiza, mokusō, recitation of dōjō kun, bows. The examination begins the moment you enter the dōjō. Composure here sets the tone for everything after.

Component 2 · 30–45 min

Kihon demonstration

All stances. Punches, blocks, kicks called by examiner. PT 1 and PT 2 forward and back. Combinations in pairs. Listen to every count; do not anticipate.

Component 3 · 30–45 min

Kata demonstration

Typically 3–6 kata required: at least one Fukyū, the Naihanchi(s), full Pinan series (often 2–3 chosen at random), one intermediate (Passai, Ananku, Wansu, or Seisan), and one tokui-gata (chosen kata at high standard — usually Passai Dai or Kūsankū).

Component 4 · 20–30 min

Kata bunkai with a partner

Selected sections of 1–2 kata performed at speed against an attacker. Often the most heavily weighted component — examiners want to see that the kata is understood, not just executed. Choose bunkai you have drilled hundreds of times.

Component 5 · 15–25 min

Kumite

Several rounds: ippon kumite, jiyū-ippon, then jiyū kumite. Many schools require the shodan gauntlet — 3 to 5 consecutive jiyū-kumite rounds against fresh senior partners. Conditioning matters here as much as technique.

Component 6 · 10–20 min

Kobudō kata

At least one weapon kata. Shūshi-no-Kon (bō) is the standard. OSK's four-weapon syllabus may also ask for sai or tonfa.

Component 7 · 15 min

Oral / written examination

  • History of Shōrin-Ryū — name the three branches and their founders.
  • Lineage of OSK Academy.
  • Recite the dōjō kun from memory in Japanese.
  • Translate the names and meanings of major kata.
  • Some schools: a written essay — "What does shodan mean to me?"

Component 8 · 5–10 min

Conditioning (sometimes a gate)

Push-ups, sit-ups, stance holds, makiwara strikes. Some schools test fitness before kata — to see what the candidate looks like under fatigue.

Component 9 · variable

Self-defense demonstration (goshin-jutsu)

Application against grabs, holds, weapons (knife, club). Increasingly common in modern Okinawan schools.

Component 10 · Close

Verdict, line-up, bow

The panel deliberates. Result is announced — sometimes immediately, sometimes by post within days. Whatever the result: bow, thank the examiners, return to training tomorrow.

Two months before the test

Visit Renshi Sajesh or Kyōshi Chandran. Ask explicitly:

  • "Which kata will I be required to perform?"
  • "Which sections will I demonstrate as bunkai?"
  • "How many kumite rounds, and against whom?"
  • "Which kobudō kata?"
  • "What is on the oral / written component?"

Sensei always outranks the page. This site is preparation; sensei is the syllabus.

当日 — Tōjitsu

The day before, the day of

Day before

  • Light training only — one full kata run-through, then rest. Do not exhaust the body.
  • Pack the gi (clean, ironed), spare belt, hand wraps, water, towel, snack.
  • Pack any kobudō weapon you will be tested on — verify it is in good order.
  • Sleep 8 hours.
  • No alcohol, no heavy food.
  • Re-read the dōjō kun and one history page.

Day of

  • Light breakfast 2 hours before. Banana + nuts mid-morning.
  • Hydrate steadily — small sips, not gulps.
  • Arrive 30 minutes early. Bow at the threshold. Greet your examiners formally.
  • Warm up alone, quietly. Joint circles, light stances, one Pinan.
  • If a technique goes wrong — recover, do not stop. The recovery is what is being tested.
  • If you forget a kata sequence — pause, breathe, restart from kamae. Better than a wild improvisation.
  • End every kata with full zanshin. Hold the final stance. Eyes forward. Then naore.
  • Whatever the result: bow, thank, leave with dignity. Train again tomorrow.
"If you have trained well, the test is the easy part. If you have not trained well, no advice will help. The day-of work was done in the months before." — a common Okinawan teaching
初段の後 — Shodan no ato

After shodan — what then?

The black belt is awarded the day after the test. Most students hear at this point what they suspected — that shodan is not a graduation, but the entry into a new kind of training. A few changes follow:

  • Bunkai becomes the main subject. Below shodan, you learn the form. After shodan, you learn what the form means.
  • Teaching becomes part of training. You are now sempai to colored belts. The act of teaching reveals what you do not yet know — which is most things.
  • Cross-style kata enter. OSK's Goju-influenced advanced kata (Seipai, Nipaipo, Shisōchin, Anan, Unsū) become accessible.
  • Kobudō deepens. Multiple weapon kata; transitions between weapon and empty-hand.
  • Self-direction grows. No one will tell you when to grade for nidan. You decide when you are ready and ask.
  • The dōjō kun starts to make sense. The five precepts are the actual curriculum from shodan onward — not symbolic words but practical disciplines, applied daily.
"Spend three years to find a teacher. Spend ten years training. Then you will know what karate is." — a Ryūkyū saying, paraphrased
