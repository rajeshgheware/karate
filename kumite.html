Kumite

Where kihon meets a real partner and kata becomes pressure-tested. Kumite in Shōrin-Ryū is a graduated ladder — beginners learn one step at a time, advanced practitioners learn one moment at a time.

六段階 — Roku-dankai

The kumite ladder — six stages

From completely scripted to completely free. Each stage adds one variable. Skip stages and you skip the lesson hidden in them.

Stage 1 · 9th–7th kyū

Kihon ippon kumite — 基本一本組手

One-step pre-arranged sparring. Attacker announces target (jōdan / chūdan) and attacks with a single named technique; defender blocks and counters with a single named technique. Both bow, attacker steps, both bow. Repeat.

Lesson: correct form under the slightest pressure of a real attacker. Builds ma-ai (distance) and clean blocks.

Stage 2 · 7th–5th kyū

Sanbon kumite — 三本組手

Three-step sparring. Attacker delivers three sequential attacks (e.g. jōdan oi-zuki, chūdan oi-zuki, mae-geri); defender retreats one step blocking each, then counters on the third.

Lesson: retreating without breaking posture; identifying the moment to switch from receiving to attacking.

Stage 3 · 5th–3rd kyū

Gohon kumite — 五本組手

Five-step sparring. The same idea as sanbon, extended to five attacks. Counter on the fifth.

Lesson: stamina under pressure; sustaining ma-ai across multiple attacks; reading the opponent's rhythm.

Stage 4 · 3rd–2nd kyū — current focus

Yakusoku kumite — 約束組手

Pre-arranged "promise" sparring. Longer, flowing scripted sequences with multiple attacks and counters, derived from kata bunkai. Each Pinan and Naihanchi has yakusoku drills; OSK Academy introduces these formally at 3rd kyū.

Lesson: kata as live application. The script is the bunkai you already know; now apply it with timing, distance, and a partner who is actually trying to land.

Stage 5 · 2nd kyū → shodan

Jiyū ippon kumite — 自由一本組手

Free one-step. Attacker chooses timing and target within an agreed technique; defender responds freely. Bridge to free sparring.

Lesson: reading intent without script. Recognizing the kamae (guard) shift that precedes the attack; choosing the counter on the spot.

Stage 6 · shodan and beyond

Jiyū kumite — 自由組手

Free sparring. Both partners attack and defend without script. Light contact in Shōrin tradition; sport karate uses point-stop (sun-dome).

Lesson: everything at once. Distance, timing, deception, recovery, breath. The grading-day "shodan gauntlet" — facing 3–5 senior partners back-to-back — sits here.

間合い — Ma-ai

Distance — the silent skill

Distance decides the fight before timing or technique do. Three ranges — and the moment you cross between them is the moment to act.

Tō-ma · 遠間

Long distance. Safe. Used to read the opponent, breathe, choose your moment. Crossing it requires intention.

Uchi-ma · 打ち間

Mid distance. Striking range with one full step. The most dangerous range — neither out nor in, every commitment is exposed.

Chika-ma · 近間

Close distance. Elbows, knees, throws, kyūsho strikes. No long techniques. The range of every old kata's bunkai.

The three moments to attack

  1. Sen no sen (先の先) — strike before they strike, having read their intent.
  2. Sen sen no sen (先々の先) — strike as they begin to strike, intercepting them mid-motion.
  3. Go no sen (後の先) — strike after they strike, having received the attack and counter-attacked on the same beat.

試合 — Shiai

Sport kumite — WKF scoring & rules

Most modern gradings include a sport-kumite component. The WKF (World Karate Federation) ruleset is the standard.

有効 · Yūkō

1 point

Any clean punch (tsuki) to a scoring zone: head, neck, abdomen, chest, back, side.

技あり · Waza-ari

2 points

Clean kick to the body (chūdan-geri).

一本 · Ippon

3 points

Kick to the head (jōdan-geri), or any scoring technique on a swept/thrown opponent.

Six criteria for a valid score

  1. Good form (yoi-keishiki) — correct technique, not a slap.
  2. Sporting attitude — non-malicious, controlled.
  3. Vigorous application — committed, not tentative.
  4. Awareness (zanshin) — composure after the technique.
  5. Good timing — at the moment of opportunity.
  6. Correct distance — close enough to be effective, controlled enough to avoid injury.

Match basics

  • Length: 3 minutes (kumite), or until 8-point lead.
  • Sun-dome: stop short of contact (or light contact with full control).
  • Mubobi: failure to protect oneself — penalized.
  • Penalties: Category 1 (excessive contact) and Category 2 (rule violations). Progress: Chūkoku → Keikoku → Hansoku-Chūi → Hansoku → Shikkaku.
  • Decisions: by points, by senshu (first unanswered point), or by hantei (judges' decision).

Sport vs. tradition — both, not either

WKF kumite trains speed, timing, and composure under pressure — invaluable. Bunkai-driven kumite trains close-range, kyūsho-aware, throw-and-strike sequences — also invaluable. The shodan grading at OSK will sample both. Train both.

心構え — Kokoro-gamae

The mind in kumite

A black-belt's body and a white-belt's body fight similarly. The black belt's mind is what makes the difference.

残心 · Zanshin

"Lingering mind." Awareness that does not collapse after the strike. After every technique, the body returns to kamae and the eyes hold the opponent.

無心 · Mushin

"No mind." Action without deliberation — the response selects itself from training. The opposite of thinking about the next move.

不動心 · Fudōshin

"Immovable mind." Calm under fear, anger, surprise. Not absence of feeling — refusal to be ruled by it.

水の心 · Mizu no kokoro

"Mind like water." A still surface reflects everything; a turbulent one reflects nothing. Read the opponent only from a calm mind.

月の心 · Tsuki no kokoro

"Mind like the moon." The moon shines on all things at once — total awareness, with no point of fixation. Avoids tunnel vision.

機 · Ki — opportunity

The instant of vulnerability — when the opponent draws breath, shifts weight, blinks. Train to perceive it; train to create it.

"Karate ni sente nashi — there is no first attack in karate. But once attacked, hesitate not." — Anko Itosu, Tōde Jukun (1908)
