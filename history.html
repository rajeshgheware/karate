Lineage & Tradition

Karate is what it is because particular men, in a particular kingdom, made particular choices. To know your art is to know their names — and the precepts they left behind.

琉球 ・ 那覇 ・ 首里 ・ 泊

Ryūkyū · Naha · Shuri · Tomari — the four cradles of karate

沖縄空手史 — Okinawa karate-shi

From Ryūkyū to "karate"

Karate did not arrive in the world fully formed. It is the slow distillation of Chinese quanfa, native Okinawan te, and political pressure into something both deadly and disciplined.

1372

Ryūkyū begins formal trade with China

Ryūkyū kingdom establishes tributary relations with Ming China. For the next four centuries, Okinawan envoys, merchants, and martial artists travel to and study in Fuzhou. Chinese quanfa begins seeping into Okinawan te.

1429

Sho Hashi unifies the Ryūkyū Islands

King Sho Hashi unifies the three kingdoms of Okinawa. Weapons become restricted to the noble (pēchin) class. Native fighting traditions consolidate around three towns — Shuri (the royal seat), Naha (the trading port), and Tomari (the harbor village).

1609

Satsuma invasion

The Satsuma clan of Japan conquers Ryūkyū. Stricter weapon bans follow. Empty-hand training becomes survival. Farmers and nobles develop the kobudō weapons (bō, sai, nunchaku, tonfa, kama, eku) from agricultural and household tools.

19th century

Three styles crystallize

Shuri-te (light, fast, evasive — Matsumura), Naha-te (heavy, rooted, breathing — Higaonna, later Goju), and Tomari-te (a blend — Matsumora). Shōrin-Ryū descends primarily from Shuri-te with Tomari-te influence.

1901–1908

Karate enters the schools

Anko Itosu introduces karate into the Okinawan public school curriculum. He creates the Pinan series (1907) for children, simplifies dangerous applications, and writes the Tōde Jūkun (Ten Precepts, 1908) — the founding document of modern karate pedagogy.

1922

Karate reaches mainland Japan

Gichin Funakoshi demonstrates karate at the First National Athletic Exhibition in Tokyo. He stays. Within a decade, karate spreads through Japanese universities under the Shōtōkan name. The kanji 唐手 ("Chinese hand") is later changed to 空手 ("empty hand").

1933

Kobayashi-Ryū named

Chōshin Chibana formally names his line of Itosu's teaching Kobayashi-Ryū (小林流) — the most directly preserved Itosu lineage.

1940

Fukyū-gata created

The Okinawan Governor's Office requests a unified beginner kata. Shōshin Nagamine creates Fukyū-gata Ichi; Chōjun Miyagi creates Fukyū-gata Ni. The first standardized teaching kata in Okinawan history.

1947

Matsubayashi-Ryū founded

Shōshin Nagamine founds Matsubayashi-Ryū (松林流, "Pine Forest Style"), honoring his two principal teachers — Bushi Matsumura (via Kyan) and Kosaku Matsumora.

1955

Ryūkyū Kobudō Hozon Shinkō Kai

Shinken Taira founds the Ryūkyū Kobudō Hozon Shinkō Kai to preserve and propagate classical Okinawan weapons traditions. Codifies the kobudō kata that spread worldwide.

1957

First 10th dan in Okinawan karate

Chōshin Chibana is awarded the first 10th dan in Okinawan karate. Confirms the formal dan-i system already widespread on the mainland.

1976

Nagamine's Essence of Okinawan Karate-do

Tuttle publishes Nagamine's monograph in English. It becomes the most-cited primary source on Shōrin-Ryū kata, dōjō kun, and lineage in the West, and remains so today.

2020

Karate at the Olympics

Karate debuts as an Olympic sport at the Tokyo 2020 Games (held 2021). Kata and kumite both contested. Although removed for Paris 2024, it returns the discipline to global awareness.

January 2, 2025

Karate begins

First class at OSK Academy. White belt, 9th kyū. The road begins. Endurance-sports background (high-altitude trekking, long-distance cycling) carries over the conditioning; only the discipline has to find a new object.

August 10, 2025 — first grading

Yellow + Blue + Purple

Triple promotion in a single test (8th → 6th kyū). Three ranks awarded on the same day on the basis of conditioning and clean form.

February 8, 2026 — second grading

Green + Brown 4 + Brown 3 — your present

A second triple promotion: 5th → 3rd kyū. Six ranks earned across two test days in thirteen months. Three grades remain (brown 2, brown 1, shodan). Roughly four centuries from the Satsuma invasion to this gi, and roughly eighteen to twenty-four months of consistent training from this rank to shodan.

先師 — Senshi

The masters of the lineage

Five generations from a king's bodyguard to a Tokyo university; the chain of teachers who carried Shōrin-Ryū to the present day.

松村 宗棍 · 1809–1899

Sōkon "Bushi" Matsumura

Bodyguard to three Ryūkyūan kings; studied under Kanga "Tōde" Sakugawa and Chinese envoys in Fuzhou. Often called the father of modern Shōrin-Ryū; codified Naihanchi, Passai, Kūsankū, Chinto, Gojūshiho, Seisan, and the legendary Hakutsuru.

His teaching synthesized Chinese quanfa with native Okinawan te. The honorific Bushi ("warrior") was conferred by the king himself.

糸洲 安恒 · 1831–1915

Anko "Yasutsune" Itosu

Senior disciple of Matsumura. The schoolteacher who introduced karate into Okinawan public schools (1901–1905). Created the Pinan series around 1907 by simplifying Kūsankū and the older Channan kata for children.

Author of the Tōde Jūkun (Ten Precepts, 1908) — a foundational document for modern karate pedagogy. Teacher of Chibana, Funakoshi, Mabuni, Kyan (briefly), and many others.

知花 朝信 · 1885–1969

Chōshin Chibana — Kobayashi-Ryū

Itosu's most senior disciple; named his style Kobayashi-Ryū ("Small Forest") in 1933 — using the same kanji as his teacher's lineage but a different reading.

Held the first 10th dan in Okinawan karate (awarded 1957). Senior students included Katsuya Miyahira, Shūgorō Nakazato, Yūchoku Higa, Chōzō Nakama. The Kobayashi line is the most directly transmitted Itosu lineage.

喜屋武 朝徳 · 1870–1945

Chōtoku Kyan — Shōbayashi-Ryū / Seibukan

Trained under Matsumura, Itosu, Oyadomari, Tokumine, and Maeda — exceptionally broad lineage. Small in stature, famous for footwork, grappling, and "live" fighting application.

His students named the line Shōbayashi-Ryū (少林流 — same kanji as Kobayashi, different reading). Zenryō Shimabukuro founded Seibukan in 1962 to preserve Kyan's curriculum. Ananku is widely attributed to Kyan.

長嶺 将真 · 1907–1997

Shōshin Nagamine — Matsubayashi-Ryū

Studied under Chōtoku Kyan, Chōki Motobu, and Ankichi Arakaki. Founded Matsubayashi-Ryū in 1947 — the "Pine Forest" reading honors his two teachers, Matsumura (via Kyan) and Matsumora.

Authored The Essence of Okinawan Karate-Do (1976) — the most-cited English-language primary source for Shōrin-Ryū kata, dōjō kun, and lineage. The 18-kata Matsubayashi curriculum is still the standard reference.

本部 朝基 · 1870–1944

Chōki Motobu

Renowned street fighter and teacher of Naihanchi. Studied under Matsumura, Itosu, and Sakuma. Famously declared "all you need is Naihanchi" and built his entire system on its principles.

Influence on Nagamine and on the Tomari-te tradition. Less institutional than his peers, but his approach to bunkai-driven application shaped modern Shōrin practice.

船越 義珍 · 1868–1957

Gichin Funakoshi — bridge to Japan

Itosu's student; the man who carried karate from Okinawa to Tokyo (1922) and established Shōtōkan at Japanese universities. Although Shōtōkan diverged stylistically, Funakoshi's philosophical writings (Niju Kun) remain shared inheritance for all Shōrin-influenced lines.

平 信賢 · 1897–1970

Shinken Taira — Kobudō

Student of Yabiku Mōden and Funakoshi. Founded the Ryūkyū Kobudō Hozon Shinkō Kai (1955) to preserve classical Okinawan weapons traditions. Authored Ryūkyū Kobudō Taikan — the standard reference for the bō, sai, nunchaku, tonfa, and kama curriculum.

道場訓 — Dōjō Kun

The Dōjō Kun — five precepts in depth

Recited at the close of every class, in seiza, eyes closed. Each precept begins with 一 ("Hitotsu" — "one") to mark them as equal in rank.

  1. 人格完成に努むること Hitotsu, jinkaku kansei ni tsutomuru koto.

    Seek perfection of character. The aim of karate is not the body, not the rank, not the trophy. It is the slow forging of jinkaku — character — which a black belt makes visible only in retrospect.

  2. 誠の道を守ること Hitotsu, makoto no michi o mamoru koto.

    Be faithful; guard the path of truth. Honesty in technique (do not perform a block you cannot apply); honesty in conduct (do not claim ranks or skills you have not earned); honesty in self-assessment (the kata never lies — you do).

  3. 努力の精神を養うこと Hitotsu, doryoku no seishin o yashinau koto.

    Cultivate the spirit of effort. Skill is the slow precipitate of consistent effort. Talent without doryoku produces nothing; doryoku without talent eventually produces everything.

  4. 礼儀を重んずること Hitotsu, reigi o omonzuru koto.

    Respect others. Reigi is not politeness — it is the structural acknowledgment that you train with humans whose well-being and dignity outrank your ego. The bow is a contract.

  5. 血気の勇を戒むること Hitotsu, kekki no yū o imashimuru koto.

    Refrain from violent and impetuous behaviour. A trained body is a weapon; an untrained mind is a fool. Skill without restraint is a moral failure. Karate ni sente nashi — there is no first attack — applies inside the dōjō and outside it.

糸洲十訓 — Itosu Jūkun

Itosu's Ten Precepts (1908)

Itosu's letter to the Okinawan Education Department in 1908 established karate's introduction into schools — and remains the founding document of modern karate pedagogy.

  1. Karate is not for warfare alone; it is for the cultivation of body and mind for daily benefit.
  2. The primary purpose of karate practice is health and self-defense, not aggression.
  3. Train the hands and feet on the makiwara; develop the body progressively.
  4. Karate practice requires perspiration daily — there are no shortcuts.
  5. Karate is the best physical exercise, building both body and indomitable spirit.
  6. Once mastered, the practitioner can face many opponents without fear.
  7. The hands and feet are weapons — use them with judgment and only when necessary.
  8. Train as if you face many opponents; perform every kata with that intention.
  9. Adjust training to your own constitution — the old, the young, the strong and the weak each train differently.
  10. Karate will help one excel in any pursuit in life.

Translation paraphrased from the standard text reproduced in Nagamine and McCarthy.

船越二十訓 — Funakoshi Niju Kun

Funakoshi's Twenty Precepts — the most-quoted five

Gichin Funakoshi articulated 20 precepts. While Funakoshi founded Shōtōkan, he was Itosu's student — and these maxims are inheritance for all Shōrin-influenced lines.

空手に先手なし

Karate ni sente nashi

"There is no first attack in karate." Defense before offense. The kata teach the principle by always opening with a block, never with a strike.

空手は義の補け

Karate wa gi no tasuke

"Karate is an aid to justice." Skill exists in service of right action, not personal gain or revenge. A karateka stands between violence and the vulnerable, never as the source of violence.

先ず自己を知れ、然して他を知れ

Mazu jiko o shire, shikoshite ta o shire

"First know yourself, then know others." The opponent in front of you is less dangerous than the opponent inside your own head. Begin with self-mastery.

技術より心術

Gijutsu yori shinjutsu

"Spirit before technique." Technique is a vessel; spirit is what fills it. A perfect kata performed without intent is empty.

常に思念工夫せよ

Tsune ni shinen kufū seyo

"Always think and devise." Perpetual study. Karate is not a body of received knowledge; it is a practice of continuous inquiry.

長嶺七則 — Nagamine Shichi-soku

Nagamine's Seven Principles

From The Essence of Okinawan Karate-Do (1976). The most concise summary of what a Shōrin-Ryū practitioner is being trained to become.

  1. Karate begins and ends with rei (courtesy).
  2. There is no first attack in karate.
  3. Karate is an assistant to justice.
  4. Know yourself before knowing others.
  5. Mind and technique become one (shin-gi-ittai).
  6. The mind needs to be set free — calm, unfixed (mushin / fudōshin).
  7. Disaster comes from carelessness — constant alertness (zanshin).
礼儀 — Reigi

Etiquette — the form of respect

"Karate-dō wa rei ni hajimari rei ni owaru." — Karate begins and ends with courtesy. Reigi is not decoration; it is the structure that makes training possible.

Bowing (rei)

Ritsurei
standing		From musubi-dachi, hands at sides; bow ~30°, eyes forward — never lose visual awareness.
Zarei
kneeling		Performed in seiza. Left knee down first, then right; rising right-first then left. Hands form a triangle on the floor.

Standard pattern at start / end of class

  1. Shōmen ni rei — bow to the front (the dōjō's symbolic spirit / kamiza).
  2. Sensei ni rei — bow to the instructor.
  3. Otagai ni rei — bow to one another (fellow students).

Entering / leaving the dōjō

  • Bow at the entrance (threshold) when entering and leaving.
  • Remove footwear before stepping onto the floor.
  • Arrive early; never arrive late. If late, kneel in seiza near the entrance and wait for sensei's signal.
  • Do not walk between two people facing each other (bowing or training).

Gi care

  • Gi clean, white, ironed, unmarked (except authorized patches).
  • The belt is never washed — tradition holds it carries the practitioner's spirit.
  • Tie obi with knot square and tails even; left side over right.
  • Fold gi properly when not worn.

Sensei / sempai relationship

  • Sensei — instructor; "one who has gone before." Address with surname + sensei.
  • Sempai — senior student. Kōhai — junior. Dōhai — peer / same rank.
  • Senior practitioners line up to the right (from the front); lower belts to the left.
  • Respond "Hai!" when called.
  • Never correct a peer in front of the class — defer to sensei.
  • Onegai shimasu ("please") at the start; arigatō gozaimashita ("thank you," past form) at the end.
補助運動 — Hojo Undō

Hojo undō — supplementary conditioning

The traditional Okinawan strength tools, performed with hand-built equipment. They build the karate body the karate kata demand.

巻藁 · Makiwara

Striking post

Wooden plank padded at striking height, anchored in the ground or wall-mounted. Conditions knuckles, trains kime (focus), and forces correct alignment of fist–wrist–elbow–shoulder–hip.

Itosu wrote in 1908 that daily makiwara practice is non-negotiable.

力石 · Chi-ishi

Strength stone

A concrete block on a wooden handle (like a single-side dumbbell). Builds wrist and grip strength, forearm rotation, shoulder stabilizers. Used in arc-swing patterns mimicking blocks and strikes.

握り瓶 · Nigiri-game

Gripping jars

Clay/ceramic jars with narrow rims, held by the fingertips. Often filled progressively with sand. Builds crushing grip, finger strength, and sanchin-stance walking endurance.

石鎖 · Ishi-sashi

Stone padlock

Kettlebell-like weight with a handle (resembles the old Okinawan padlock). Pressing, swinging, grip — analogous to modern kettlebell work.

金剛券 · Kongōken

Iron oval ring

Heavy iron oval ring (~30+ kg). Introduced to karate by Chōjun Miyagi from Hawaiian wrestling. Whole-body lifting, pressing, throwing simulation. Iconic Goju-Ryū tool.

タン / 差 · Tan / Sashi

Okinawan barbell

Wooden pole with stone weights on each end. Pressing, curling, twisting; wrist and shoulder integration.

鉄下駄 · Tetsu-geta

Iron sandals

Worn on the feet during stance work and slow kicks. Builds leg power, stance endurance, kicking strength.

鉄亜鈴 · Tetsu-arei

Iron dumbbells

General strength; integrated into kata practice (slow kata with weighted hands builds rooting and breath together).
