Kobudō

The Ryūkyū farmer-noble's arsenal — staff, sai, flail, and side-handle baton — refined into kata that sharpen the empty-hand body. Weapons of necessity, not aggression. Each weapon's demonstration clip is reference footage from an Okinawan kobudō lineage — confirm the OSK version with Sensei.

Bō · Sai · Nunchaku · Tonfa

起源 — Kigen

Why kobudō, why now

Okinawan kobudō (古武道, "old martial way") evolved alongside karate in the Ryūkyū kingdom — sometimes from the same teachers, almost always for the same people. When swords were restricted under Satsuma rule, the Ryūkyū population turned ordinary tools — boat oars, threshing flails, millstones, horse bridles — into weapons of last resort.

The four weapons in OSK Academy's syllabus belong to the classical Ryūkyū Kobudō tradition codified in the 20th century by Shinken Taira (Ryūkyū Kobudō Hozon Shinkō Kai, 1955) and Moden Yabiku, with the Matayoshi line preserving a parallel tradition. Practising kobudō does not subtract from empty-hand karate — it deepens it. The weapon is heavier than the hand; what the weapon teaches the body to do, the empty hand learns to do faster.

"If you understand the bō, you understand the body. The staff has no muscle of its own — every motion you give it must come from your hara." — attributed to Shinken Taira
棒 — Bō

The staff — Rokushaku-bō

Karate Dōjō waKu · Shūshi-no-Kon bō kata (Okinawa)

Rokushaku-bō · 六尺棒 · “six-foot staff” · ~182 cm, ~3 cm diameter · hardwood (red oak / Okinawan ironwood eku), tapered or straight.

Grips

Honte-mochi
natural grip		Palms opposing each other on the staff. Standard ready and most striking.
Gyakute-mochi
reverse grip		One palm reversed for thrusting and certain blocks.
Standard readyHands shoulder-width apart, dividing the staff one-third / two-thirds. The longer end is the working end.

Signature techniques

  • Tsuki — thrust. Held one-handed at full extension; the longest reaching attack.
  • Uchi — strike. Overhead, side, and figure-8 strikes.
  • Uke — block. Diagonal, vertical, horizontal — the staff is essentially a long rigid arm.
  • Furi — swing/whip. Full-body rotational strikes; uses the staff's entire mass.

釵 — Sai

The trident-truncheon — Sai

Nakamoto Mamoru · Chatan Yara-no-Sai (Okinawa World Tournament)

~50 cm total · octagonal or round shaft · carried in pairs (often three). Parts: monouchi (shaft ~38 cm), yoku (side prongs ~7 cm), moto/tsuka (handle).

Grips

HontePoint forward. Used for thrusting (nuki-zuki) and most strikes.
GyakutePoint backward, hugged to the forearm. Defensive carry — the sai becomes a forearm shield. Flip into honte for the strike (furi-gaeshi).
AnchorThe moto sits in the palm; the index finger braces the central shaft against the thumb side.

Signature techniques

  • Tsuki / nuki-zuki — straight thrust with the point of the monouchi.
  • Uchi — strike with either the moto (pommel) or the shaft.
  • Uke — trap and parry with the yoku prongs. The sai is famously designed to catch and disarm a bō.
  • Furi-gaeshi — flipping the sai from gyakute to honte mid-motion.

Classical sai kata

The sai is a defender, not a sword

Despite the spear-like point, classical sai work prioritizes trapping and disarming a longer weapon (bō, sword, spear). The point is real — but the prongs are why the sai exists.

双節棍 — Nunchaku

The flail — Nunchaku

Nakamoto Masahiro Hanshi · Maezato-no-Nunchaku (Okinawa)

Nunchaku · 双節棍 · “two-section stick” · two ~30 cm hardwood sticks joined by a ~10 cm cord (himo). Octagonal section is traditional Okinawan; round is modern.

Grips & technique

  • Honte — primary hand holds one stick (the working end); the other swings free.
  • Both-hands-on-one — for blocking with the stick laid across the forearms.
  • Scissor / pinch (hasami) — both sticks gripped with the cord in between, used for trapping a limb.
  • Under-arm hold — the free end tucked under the armpit between strikes.

Signature techniques

  • Furi-uchi — swinging strike. The iconic technique; whole-body rotation.
  • Uchi-komi — direct hammering strike with the working end.
  • Hasami — scissor / pinch trap (catching a limb between the two sticks).
  • Kake-uke — hooking block using the cord across an opponent's wrist.
  • Tsuki — thrust with the end of one stick.

Classical kata

Maezato-no-NunchakuCommon beginner form (Taira / Akamine line).
Taira-no-NunchakuNamed for Shinken Taira; intermediate.

Forget Bruce Lee

Despite the cinematic image, the Okinawan nunchaku is primarily a trapping and close-range weapon, not a flashy spinning tool. The cord (himo) traps wrists and locks joints; the sticks strike at very close range. Long spinning chains are demonstration, not application.

トンファー — Tonfa / Tuifa

The side-handle baton — Tonfa

Nakamoto · Hamahiga-no-Tonfa (Okinawa World Tournament)

Tonfa · トンファー · ~50 cm shaft with a perpendicular tsuka (handle) ~15 cm from one end · hardwood, used in pairs. Possibly from a millstone handle (debated).

Grips

  • Honte (default defensive) — hand on the perpendicular tsuka; short end forward, long end running along the forearm. The shaft becomes a forearm extension.
  • Gyakute — long end forward for thrusting and reaching strikes.
  • Released spin — releasing the shaft to whip out, rotating around the handle, and re-grasping. The basis of furi-uchi.

Signature techniques

  • Uke — forearm-aligned blocks. Most blocks in tonfa kata are direct extensions of empty-hand age-uke / soto-uke.
  • Uchi-komi — swinging strikes by releasing and rotating the shaft.
  • Tsuki — thrust with the long end (in gyakute).
  • Furi-uchi — full-rotation strike, building momentum from a relaxed wrist.

Classical kata

Hamahiga-no-TonfaThe most common classical tonfa form across kobudō schools.
Yaraguwā-no-TonfaIntermediate; energetic with multiple direction changes.

The tonfa lives on

The tonfa is the only classical Okinawan weapon still in worldwide use as a duty weapon — adopted by police forces (the "PR-24" / side-handle baton) for exactly the reason it was designed: a forearm-shielding striking tool that requires no edge or projectile.

原則 — Gensoku

Five principles common to all four weapons

1. The body powers the weapon, never the arm.

A bō swung from the arm is a stick. A bō swung from the hara is a hammer. The hip must initiate every strike.

2. The weapon is an extension of the empty-hand technique.

Bō age-uchi is age-uke with a stick. Sai tsuki is gyaku-zuki with a point. Tonfa-uchi is uchi-uke at full extension. Every weapon kata maps onto karate.

3. Grip changes are technique transitions.

Furi-gaeshi (sai), nunchaku hand-switch, tonfa release-and-grab — the moment of grip change is also the moment of attack.

4. The weapon protects the body from the body.

Holding a heavy implement enforces correct alignment — wrist, elbow, shoulder must all line up or the weapon punishes you. Kobudō discovers errors that empty-hand training hides.

5. Footwork outranks everything.

A bō user who cannot move quickly is just a target with a long stick. The same stances and tai-sabaki as karate — only more disciplined, because the weapon does not forgive.

For shodan

OSK Academy will likely require at least one weapon kata at shodan — typically Shūshi-no-Kon (bō), with optional Hamahiga-no-Tonfa or a sai kata. Begin the bō kata now.
