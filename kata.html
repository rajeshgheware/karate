Forms · 型

Kata

Kata is the textbook of karate. Each form is a record of one master's solution to one type of problem. Performed alone, the kata is shape; performed against a partner (bunkai), it becomes lesson.

型 ・ 分解 ・ 演武線

Kata · Bunkai · Embusen — Form · Application · Floor pattern

Which of these are required for shodan?

This page is the full lifelong kata library. The official OSK 2026 shodan grading requires a narrower set: Taikyoku Shodan, Fukyū 1 & 2, Naihanchi Shodan & Nidan, the five Pinan, Passai Shō, and one tournament kata. The advanced forms below — Chinto, Kūsankū, Passai Dai, Seisan, Gojūshiho, Rōhai, Jion, Ananku, and the Goju/Ryūei cross-style kata — come after shodan, in the nidan years and beyond. Train them for depth, but don't mistake them for the shodan requirement.

読み方 — Yomi-kata

How to read a kata page

Three layers of understanding

  1. Omote (表) — the surface form. The shape, sequence, breath, kiai. What an examiner sees.
  2. Bunkai (分解) — the application. What each move means against a partner — strike, throw, joint lock, escape.
  3. Ōyō (応用) — the spirit. How the kata's principle (rooting, deception, angular footwork, breath) carries into your other techniques and the rest of life.

An examiner can show all three. A student rehearses surface; a black belt rehearses bunkai; a master lives ōyō.

The embusen — floor pattern

Every kata is performed on a fixed pattern called the embusen. It encodes how an attacker (or attackers) approaches you. Recognizing the embusen is the first step in unpacking bunkai.

I — straight line
Naihanchi, Seisan, Ananku

H / +
Pinan, Kūsankū

Diagonal
Chinto only

Three-times rule

Master Funakoshi taught: every kata should be performed three times in a row. Once for shape (slow, exact). Once for breath (with kiai, finding the rhythm). Once for application (mentally rehearsing each bunkai partner). Repeated daily, three reps of one kata reveals more than thirty reps of three.

普及型 — Fukyū-gata

Foundational kata — Taikyoku, Fukyū & Naihanchi

The "promotional" kata of 1940 were created jointly by Nagamine and Miyagi to give Okinawan schoolchildren a single accessible entry into karate. Naihanchi was once the entire syllabus of Shuri-te. The OSK syllabus opens even earlier, with Taikyoku Shodan.

Taikyoku Shodan 太極 初段

"First Cause / Great Ultimate, First Level" — Funakoshi's introductory form

~20 moves Embusen: I / H Funakoshi, c.1940

H-shape

The simplest classical form — only gedan-barai and oi-zuki, on an H pattern. Created to drill stance, turning, and the basic block-punch reflex before any technical kata. The first kata on the OSK grading list.

Key techniques: zenkutsu-dachi gedan-barai · oi-zuki chūdan · 90° and 180° turns
Bunkai
  1. Gedan-barai sweeps a low attack while the turn takes you off the line.
  2. Oi-zuki pursues — the whole form is "receive, turn, counter," repeated in every direction.
  3. Its real lesson is transitions: clean turns and consistent stance depth, not the techniques themselves.

Taikyoku Shodan — beginner kata (Funakoshi lineage)

Fukyū-gata Ichi 普及型 一

"Promotional / Universal Form #1"

~21 moves Embusen: I Created 1940 · Nagamine

straight line

Created at the request of the Okinawan Governor's Office to standardize beginner instruction across schools. The first kata most Shōrin students ever perform.

Key techniques: zenkutsu-dachi gedan-barai · oi-zuki chūdan · 180° turns with simultaneous block-and-strike
Bunkai
  1. Opening gedan-barai jams a low front kick while stepping off-line.
  2. Oi-zuki chains pursue a retreating opponent in a straight corridor.
  3. 180° turns teach awareness of attackers behind you — the spine remains the axis.

Eihachi Ota Hanshi · Matsubayashi-Ryū

Fukyū-gata Ni 普及型 二

"Promotional Form #2"

~22 moves Embusen: I / shallow H Created 1940 · Miyagi

I with side step

Created in parallel with Fukyū Ichi by Chōjun Miyagi (Goju-Ryū founder). In Goju lines often called Gekisai Dai Ichi; Shōrin schools sometimes re-choreograph it.

Key techniques: jōdan age-uke · mae-geri · gyaku-zuki · shutō-uke in kōkutsu-dachi
Bunkai
  1. Age-uke + reverse punch = parry a high committed punch and counter to the ribs.
  2. Front kick + punch combo = closing distance against a retreating opponent.
  3. Shutō-uke in cat stance = transitioning to a neutral guard, ready to counter-strike.

Kyūdōkan · Okinawa Shōrin-Ryū

Fukyū-gata San 普及型 三

"Promotional Form #3"

~20–25 moves Embusen: H / + School-specific

cross / four-direction

Not part of Nagamine's original Matsubayashi 18 — taught in some Okinawan and OSMKKF lines as an extension. Verify the OSK version with sensei before grading.

Key techniques: uchi-uke + gyaku-zuki combo · yoko-geri keage · hiza-geri · double-step turns
Bunkai
  1. Block-strike-pull = receive, anchor, and counter on a single beat.
  2. Side kick after a turn = controlling lateral attackers.
  3. Knee strike = close-range finisher after a clinch.

Matsubayashi-Ryū

Naihanchi Shodan 内畔戦 初段

"Inner Divided Conflict, First Level" — alternatively "Holding Your Ground" / "Iron Horse" (Tekki in Shōtōkan)

~28 moves Embusen: I (sideways) Pre-Matsumura · Itosu split

sideways straight line

The foundational kata of Shuri-te. Motobu Chōki famously declared "all you need is Naihanchi." Performed entirely in naihanchi-dachi on a single sideways line — practitioners spend years in this kata before moving to others.

Key techniques: nami-gaeshi (returning wave kick) · kagi-zuki (hook punch) · haishu-uchi (back-hand strike) · combined uchi-uke + soto-uke double block · double-arm sweep
Bunkai
  1. The side-on stance = fighting with back to a wall, in a doorway, or on a narrow bridge — there is nowhere to retreat.
  2. Kagi-zuki = striking an opponent who has clinched at close range — the elbow stays trapped against your own ribs.
  3. Nami-gaeshi = stomping/lifting to defend against a low kick or break a leg-grab.
  4. Combined block + strike = the same arm receives and attacks on the same beat (efficient for close work).

Naihanchi Nidan & Sandan were created by Itosu by splitting the older single Naihanchi. Nidan (its own card below) is required for the shodan grading; Sandan — rapid double techniques and trapping motions — typically comes after.

Shidōkan Masters · Maeshiro / Takara / Nakamura

Naihanchi Nidan 内畔戦 二段

"Inner Divided Conflict, Second Level" — Itosu's split from the original Naihanchi

~24 moves Embusen: I (sideways) Itosu

sideways straight line

The second Naihanchi, required for the shodan grading. It keeps the sideways line and naihanchi-dachi of Shodan but adds double-handed blocking, rising-elbow motions, and a more upright, pressing rhythm. Shorter and starker than Shodan.

Key techniques: morote-uke (double-arm block) · rising elbow (hiji-ate) · uchi-uke + tsuki on one beat · nami-gaeshi returning-wave kick
Bunkai
  1. Double-handed blocks trap a grabbing or punching arm between both forearms — set up for a break or pull.
  2. Rising elbow answers an opponent who has closed to clinch range against the side-on body.
  3. Same side-on principle as Shodan: fighting where there is no room to retreat — a wall, a doorway, a crowd.

Okinawa Shidōkan · Shōrin-Ryū

平安五段 — Pinan Go-dan

The Pinan series — five forms of "Peaceful Mind"

Created by Anko Itosu around 1907 by simplifying Kūsankū and Channan, so that karate could be taught to schoolchildren. All five share the same H-embusen. They are the heart of brown-belt training.

Note: in Shōrin-Ryū the order is Itosu's original — Pinan Shodan first, then Nidan, etc. Shōtōkan reversed Shodan and Nidan (calling them Heian Nidan and Shodan). What Shōrin calls "Pinan Shodan" is what Shōtōkan calls "Heian Nidan."

Pinan Shodan 平安 初段

"Peaceful Mind, First Level"

~21 movesEmbusen: HItosu, c.1907

H-shape

Key techniques: shutō-uke in kōkutsu-dachi (signature opening) · nukite (spear-hand) · age-uke · oi-zuki chūdan
Bunkai
  1. Opening shutō sequence = parry-grab-strike against a lapel grab.
  2. Nukite to solar plexus = vital-point thrust after pulling the opponent's guard down with hikite.
  3. The H pattern teaches angular footwork — never receive an attack straight on; always step off-line.

Shidōkan Masters · Maeshiro / Takara / Nakamura

Pinan Nidan 平安 二段

"Peaceful Mind, Second Level"

~26 movesEmbusen: H

H-shape

Key techniques: age-uke chain · mae-geri · ren-zuki (consecutive punches) · shutō-uke turn
Bunkai
  1. Rising block + counterpunch combinations against a series of straight attacks.
  2. The mae-geri line teaches engagement at correct ma-ai — hit the moment the opponent enters your kicking range.
  3. Final ren-zuki = closing aggressively after a successful counter, denying the opponent recovery.

Shidōkan Masters · Maeshiro / Takara / Nakamura

Pinan Sandan 平安 三段

"Peaceful Mind, Third Level"

~20 movesEmbusen: H + rear empi

H + rear elbow turn

Key techniques: uchi-uke + gyaku-zuki combo · double block (morote-uke) · tetsui (hammer-fist) on rear turn · stepping elbow strike (empi)
Bunkai
  1. The closing 180° turn with empi = throwing or unbalancing an opponent grabbing from behind.
  2. Repeated uchi-uke + gyaku-zuki teaches simultaneous block-and-counter — same beat, no pause.
  3. Double block traps an opponent's punching limb between both forearms for an arm break.

Shidōkan Masters · Maeshiro / Takara / Nakamura

Pinan Yondan 平安 四段

"Peaceful Mind, Fourth Level"

~27 movesEmbusen: H

H + jūji-uke (X-block)

Key techniques: kosa-uke / jūji-uke (X-block) · mae-geri · mawashi-empi (round elbow) · shutō-uke chain · hiza-geri (knee strike)
Bunkai
  1. X-block intercepts a club, bottle, or downward weapon attack — wrists support each other.
  2. Elbow + grab + knee combo simulates a clinch: pull head down, drive knee up.
  3. Teaches transitions between long and close range — Pinan Yondan is the bridge to Passai.

Shidōkan Masters · Maeshiro / Takara / Nakamura

Pinan Godan 平安 五段

"Peaceful Mind, Fifth Level"

~23 movesEmbusen: H + jumpMost demanding Pinan

H + jumping turn

Key techniques: mikazuki-geri (crescent kick, in some versions) · jūji-uke gedan · jumping turn · shutō-uchi
Bunkai
  1. The jump-turn = evading a sweep or low attack while reorienting to a new opponent behind.
  2. Final jūji-uke teaches a wrist-grab release — wrists form a fulcrum to free a trapped hand.
  3. Considered the most technically demanding Pinan; mastery is often the milestone for brown 1.

Lineage variant: Matsubayashi-Ryū often omits the jump and substitutes a stepped turn; Kobayashi and Shitō-Ryū preserve the jump.

Shidōkan Masters · Maeshiro / Takara / Nakamura

中級型 — Chūkyū-gata

Intermediate kata — Wansū, Seisan, Ananku, Passai, Rōhai, Jion

After the Pinans, kata begin to specialize: throwing (Wansū), rooting (Seisan), aggressive entry (Passai), single-leg balance (Rōhai), high-power patterns (Jion). These define brown-belt depth.

Wansū 汪輯 — Empi in Shōtōkan

Named after Wang Ji (Wanshū), a Chinese envoy who reached Tomari in 1683 — nicknamed the "dumping form" for its signature throw

~30 movesEmbusen: I / shallow HTomari-te · Kyan lineage

I with side step

A Tomari-te kata carried into Shōrin-Ryū through Chōtoku Kyan (who learned it from Maeda Chiku). Short but technically demanding — built around simultaneous block-and-counter and its trademark kata-guruma (shoulder / "flying-swallow") throw. In the OSK syllabus it is taught directly after Pinan Godan.

Key techniques: kata-guruma throw ("flying swallow") · morote sukui-uke (double scooping block) · simultaneous uke + counter · low crouch / duck-under · shutō-uke
Bunkai
  1. The signature throw = catch a committed attacker, load them across your shoulders/arm, and dump them — the "flying swallow."
  2. Low crouch / duck-under = slipping beneath a high strike or escaping a head-grab to set up the throw.
  3. Block-and-counter on one beat = the same arm receives and strikes together, the hallmark of Wansū's tempo.

Shōrin-Ryū Seibukan · Kyan lineage

Seisan 十三 / 十三手

"Thirteen" or "Thirteen Hands" — variously: 13 techniques, 13 enemies, or auspicious Chinese numerology

~32–37 movesEmbusen: IOne of the oldest kata

straight line

One of the oldest extant kata — versions exist in Goju, Uechi, and every Shōrin lineage. The Matsumura version is the Shōrin standard. Trains rooting, breath, and rotational power.

Key techniques: sanchin-like breath/pressure · double punch (morote-zuki) · tate-shutō-uke · mae-geri keage · age-uke + gyaku-zuki finish
Bunkai
  1. Opening triple sequence = receiving, controlling, and counter-striking a committed lunge punch.
  2. The pulling hand (hikite) emphasizes off-balancing the opponent's wrist — every pull is a setup for a strike.
  3. The kata trains rooting and breath against shoving/pushing attacks. Often used as the partner kata for Sanchin training.

Shōei Miyazato · Shōrin-Ryū Shidōkan

Ananku アーナンクー

Often translated "Light from the South" — a reference to its Taiwanese origin

~26 movesEmbusen: I / shallow TKyan, ~1895

straight + side step

Created by Chōtoku Kyan after a study trip to Taiwan around 1895 — unique among classical Okinawan forms for being the youngest. A signature kata of the Shōbayashi/Seibukan line.

Key techniques: zenkutsu oi-zuki opening · double shutō-uke · yoko-geri kekomi · mawashi-zuki
Bunkai
  1. Opening forward charge = pre-emptive offense — moving first when you have read the attacker's intent.
  2. Double shutō = simultaneous two-handed neck attack, or breaking out of a clinch.
  3. Side kick + reverse punch = lateral counter against an opponent flanking you.

Shōrin-Ryū Seibukan · Kyan lineage

Passai Shō 抜砦 小

"Penetrating the Fortress, Minor Form" — also "To extract from a fortress"

~22 movesEmbusen: I + side pivotsItosu

I + side pivots

Itosu created Passai Shō by simplifying Matsumura's older Passai. Designed to teach bo-disarm sequences against a staff-armed attacker.

Key techniques: opening morote-uke (augmented block) entry · sukui-uke (scooping block) · bo-disarm sequence · nukite
Bunkai
  1. Augmented block = entering against a heavy weapon (bo) attack — the supporting hand reinforces the receiving forearm.
  2. Scooping block + grab + counter = capturing the bo and reversing it on the attacker.
  3. Final lunge teaches the commitment required to "storm a fortress" — once you commit, do not retreat.

Shidōkan Masters · Koryū Passai

Passai (Dai) 抜砦 大

"Penetrating the Fortress, Major Form"

~42 movesEmbusen: I + 45° pivotsMatsumura's original

I + 45° entries

Matsumura's original "Passai." Two versions exist in lineage — Matsumura-no-Passai and Itosu-no-Passai — differing in details. A formidable, strong-spirited kata; widely used as a tokui-gata (chosen kata) for shodan exams.

Key techniques: shoulder-tackle entry · double uchi-uke · yama-zuki (mountain punch) · shutō-uke kōkutsu · sweep + downward punch
Bunkai
  1. Initial charge = closing distance against a stronger opponent ("storming the fortress" — overwhelm in a single beat).
  2. Yama-zuki = simultaneous two-level attack: face and ribs at once.
  3. The 45° entries teach off-line angles — never face the attacker square once contact is made.

Shidōkan Masters · Matsumura no Passai

Gojūshiho 五十四歩

"54 Steps." Also called Useishi in Okinawan, Hotaku ("Woodpecker") in Matsumura Seitō

~54–67 movesEmbusen: I or +Advanced

cross / four-direction

Attributed to Matsumura, refined by Itosu (some lineages have Shō/Dai versions). Famous for the woodpecker rhythm of rapid pecking strikes (keitō, washide, ippon-nukite).

Key techniques: ippon-nukite (one-finger spear-hand) · keitō-uchi (chicken-head wrist strike) · tate-zuki · washide (eagle-hand) · repeated nukite chains
Bunkai
  1. Nukite chains = vital-point attacks (eyes, throat, suigetsu) — pinpoint, not power.
  2. The "woodpecker" name reflects the rapid pecking strikes of the keitō and washide forms.
  3. Considered an advanced kata teaching pinpoint precision — kata for the brown belt aspiring to shodan.

Maeshiro Morinobu Hanshi · Shōrin-Ryū Shidōkan

Rōhai 鷺牌 / 老鶴

"Vision of a Heron" (Matsubayashi) or "Old Crane" (Itosu/Mabuni)

~28 movesEmbusen: T / ITomari-te roots · Itosu redacted three versions

T-shape

Tomari-te kata via Kosaku Matsumora; Itosu redacted three versions (Shō/Dai/Ni). Famous for the sagi-ashi-dachi — the one-legged crane stance.

Key techniques: sagi-ashi-dachi (heron stance) · tomoe-zuki (twin punches in opposing directions) · gedan-barai · mawashi-uke
Bunkai
  1. One-leg stance = checking or attacking the opponent's lead leg; can also be a knee strike chambered.
  2. The crane motif represents balance and precision over power — the kata of the older karateka.
  3. Tomoe-zuki = two attackers, one in front and one behind, struck on the same beat.

Shōrin-Ryū Bushidaikan

Jion 慈恩

"Mercy and Goodness" — possibly named after the Jion Temple in China

~47 movesEmbusen: I + strong +Tomari-te / Shitō-Ryū origin

cross / four-direction

Originally Tomari-te / Chinese tradition; preserved primarily in Shōtōkan, Shitō-Ryū, and Wadō-Ryū. Less common in pure Shōrin lineages — its presence in OSK signals cross-style training.

Key techniques: opening morote-uke in kiba-dachi after kuma-de (bear-hand) salutation · kosa-uke · jōdan age-uke + gyaku-zuki chain · augmented punch
Bunkai
  1. Opening salutation = a Buddhist Shaolin greeting indicating non-aggression — the kata begins by offering peace.
  2. Augmented blocks = receiving heavy committed strikes by reinforcing the blocking arm with the opposite hand.
  3. Cross-pattern footwork teaches multiple-opponent management — every direction is an attacker.

Shōrin-Ryū Shidōkan

上級型 — Jōkyū-gata

Advanced kata — Chinto, Kūsankū, and beyond

The kata of the post-shodan curriculum — not required for the OSK 2026 shodan grading. Chinto and Kūsankū are typically the first kata added after black belt; Seipai, Nīpaipo, Unsū, Anan, Shisōchin follow in the nidan years.

Chinto 鎮東 — Gankaku in Shōtōkan

"Fighter to the East" — named for a Chinese sailor who taught Matsumura

~33 movesDiagonal embusen — uniqueMatsumura → Kyan

diagonal 45° line — unique

The only major Shōrin kata performed on a diagonal line. Legend places its origin on a beach — fighting on uneven ground. Famous for tsuru-ashi-dachi, the crane stance.

Key techniques: tsuru-ashi-dachi (crane stance) · jumping kick (tobi-geri) · simultaneous block + kick · ren-geri (consecutive kicks)
Bunkai
  1. The diagonal embusen represents fighting on uneven ground — sand, rocks, slopes.
  2. Crane stance = evading sweeps and attacks to the support leg; the standing leg is also a counter weapon.
  3. Jumping kick teaches commitment, recovery, and zanshin — landing must be balanced enough to attack again.

Shidōkan Masters (titled Gangaku)

Kūsankū Shō / Dai 公相君 — Kankū in Shōtōkan

Named for the Chinese envoy "Kong Su Kung" who visited Okinawa in 1756

Shō ~48Dai ~65Embusen: +Night-fighting kata

+ (cross), four-direction

Kūsankū → Sakugawa → Matsumura. Itosu created the Shō version; the Dai is Matsumura's original. Famously a night-combat treatise — many techniques described as fighting in low light.

Key techniques: opening kankū salutation (looking through the triangular hand frame) · low duck and one-handed attack sequences · jumping kick at end · shutō chains
Bunkai
  1. The opening salute may have been a range/light gauge — peering through the frame at the opponent's outline.
  2. Low postures simulate fighting in darkness — when you cannot see, you stay low and let the attacker reach for you.
  3. Jumping kick at the end is an escape-and-counter when surrounded.
  4. Considered the longest and most comprehensive of Itosu's lineage kata.

分解の原則 — Bunkai no gensoku

Eight principles for unpacking bunkai

When sensei is not in the room, these principles let you guess at meaning — usually correctly.

1. Every block is also a strike.

An uke hits the limb hard enough to stop or break it. Look for which body weapon the blocking arm uses on impact.

2. The pulling hand is grabbing something.

Hikite is never empty. It is gripping the opponent's wrist, sleeve, hair, or testicles, and pulling them off-balance.

3. Turns are throws or unbalances.

A 180° pivot is the moment a partner leaves the floor. The "turn" rotates the opponent, not you.

4. Stances are transitions, not destinations.

You arrive in a stance because you have just thrown someone, broken a balance, or stepped off-line — not because the stance is the goal.

5. Two arms moving together = one technique on one limb.

Morote-uke, augmented blocks, double-handed motions — both arms are usually working on the same arm of the opponent.

6. Kiai marks application, not noise.

Where the kata kiais is where the killing technique sits. Bunkai it carefully.

7. Symmetry is illusion.

Kata performs both sides for training. In application, only one side is happening — and the "other side" is what the partner is doing.

8. If it cannot be done full-speed against a resisting partner, the bunkai is wrong.

Keep iterating. Real bunkai survives sparring; flowery bunkai does not.
