Terminology

Japanese is the operating language of the dōjō. Knowing it lets you respond to commands without translation lag, understand a sensei from any country, and read the original sources rather than someone's interpretation of them.

"Shodan" 初段

Often translated "first-degree black belt." More accurately: "first step." The character 初 means "beginning"; 段 means "step / level." Shodan is the door to serious training, not a graduation.

漢字 — Kanji

The kanji you see most

Eight characters that sit on dōjō walls, makimono scrolls, and certificate borders. Recognize them and you can read most of what surrounds you.

道

Dō / Michi

"The Way." Karate-dō, judō, kendō — the philosophical path, not just the technique.

空

Kara / Kū

"Empty." As in karate (空手, "empty hand"). Originally written 唐手 ("Chinese hand"); changed by Funakoshi.

手

Te / Shu

"Hand." Karate, te (the original Okinawan word), shutō ("knife-hand").

心

Shin / Kokoro

"Mind / heart." Mushin, fudōshin, zanshin — the disciplines of the trained mind.

技

Gi / Waza

"Technique." Waza is the unit of action — uchi-waza, geri-waza, nage-waza.

体

Tai / Karada

"Body." Shin-gi-tai (心技体) — the mind-technique-body trinity karateka aim to integrate.

禮

Rei

"Courtesy / bow." Karate begins and ends with rei. Modern simplified form: 礼.

力

Riki / Chikara

"Power / strength." Not muscle — the integrated whole-body force that comes from rooted stance and aligned hip.
